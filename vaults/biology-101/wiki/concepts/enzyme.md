# Enzyme

An **enzyme** is a biological catalyst that speeds up a chemical reaction without being used up in the process. Most enzymes are [[protein|proteins]], folded into precise three-dimensional shapes, though a few are made of RNA. Cells depend on enzymes because the reactions of life, such as breaking down food or copying DNA, would otherwise occur far too slowly to sustain a living organism. By accelerating these reactions enormously, often making them millions of times faster, enzymes let a cell control exactly which chemistry happens and when.

Enzymes work by lowering the **activation energy**, the initial energy barrier that a reaction must overcome before it can proceed. Each enzyme has a region called the active site, a pocket whose shape and chemical properties fit a particular reactant, called the substrate. This snug fit gives enzymes their specificity: a given enzyme usually catalyzes just one reaction or a small family of related ones. Once the substrate binds, the enzyme strains, positions, or stabilizes it so the reaction occurs more easily, then releases the products and is ready to act again.

An enzyme's activity depends on conditions like temperature and acidity, and on regulatory molecules that turn it up or down, which is one way cells maintain [[homeostasis]]. Many enzymes also drive reactions that would not happen on their own by coupling them to the energy stored in [[atp]]. The orchestrated action of thousands of different enzymes underlies metabolism as a whole, including the energy-releasing steps of [[cellular-respiration]].
