# Translation

**Translation** is the process that converts the coded message in a strand of [[rna]] into an actual chain of amino acids. It is the second major step of gene expression, following transcription, and it is where genetic information finally becomes a working molecule. During translation, the cell reads the messenger RNA in groups of three bases. Each three-letter unit is called a codon, and each codon specifies a particular amino acid, the building block of a [[protein]].

The reading is carried out by the [[ribosome]], a large complex of RNA and protein that clamps onto the messenger and moves along it codon by codon. The matching of codons to amino acids depends on transfer RNA. Each transfer RNA carries one specific amino acid and displays a matching three-base sequence, an anticodon, that recognizes its codon by complementary base pairing. As the ribosome advances, it brings the correct transfer RNAs into place and links their amino acids together with peptide bonds, forming a growing chain called a polypeptide.

Translation begins at a start codon, continues as the ribosome adds amino acid after amino acid, and ends when it reaches a stop codon that signals release of the finished chain. The new polypeptide then folds into its functional shape. Through this elegant decoding, the abstract sequence of a gene is realized as a real molecule, completing the journey described in [[from-dna-to-protein]] from stored instructions to living chemistry.
