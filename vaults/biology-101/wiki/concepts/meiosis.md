# Meiosis

**Meiosis** is the specialized form of cell division that produces gametes, the reproductive cells such as sperm and eggs. Unlike ordinary division, meiosis reduces the chromosome number by half, transforming a single diploid cell into four haploid cells. This reduction is essential for sexual reproduction, because when two gametes fuse during fertilization, the resulting offspring regains the full diploid number rather than doubling it each generation. To achieve this, the cell carries out two consecutive rounds of division, called meiosis I and meiosis II, after copying its [[chromosome]] set just once.

The first division is where the dramatic genetic shuffling occurs. Matching pairs of chromosomes, one inherited from each parent, come together and can exchange segments in a process called crossing over, which recombines genetic material in new combinations. The way these chromosome pairs orient themselves before separating is essentially random, a principle known as independent assortment. Together, crossing over and independent assortment generate enormous diversity, ensuring that no two gametes are likely to be identical. This variation is the raw material on which heredity and inheritance act, the very patterns first quantified by [[gregor-mendel]].

The second division resembles ordinary nuclear division, separating the sister chromatids so that each of the four final cells receives a single, complete set. The contrast with [[mitosis]] is instructive: mitosis preserves the genome unchanged across two identical cells, while meiosis deliberately halves and reshuffles it across four distinct ones. These complementary strategies are compared in detail in [[how-cells-divide]], and the variation meiosis produces underlies much of the diversity that biology depends upon.
