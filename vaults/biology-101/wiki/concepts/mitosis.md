# Mitosis

**Mitosis** is the process by which a eukaryotic cell divides its nucleus to produce two daughter cells that are genetically identical to the original parent cell. It is the engine of growth, tissue repair, and asexual reproduction in many organisms. Because each new cell must carry a complete and accurate copy of the genome, mitosis is always preceded by [[dna-replication]], during which every [[chromosome]] is copied so that it consists of two identical sister chromatids joined together. The actual division is then a carefully choreographed sorting of these chromatids.

Biologists divide mitosis into a continuous sequence of phases. During prophase, the loosely organized genetic material condenses into compact, visible chromosomes, and the spindle apparatus begins to form. In metaphase, the chromosomes line up single file across the middle of the cell. During anaphase, the sister chromatids are pulled apart toward opposite poles, ensuring each end of the cell receives one full set. Finally, in telophase, new nuclear envelopes form around the two separated sets, and the chromosomes relax again. This pulling and sorting depends on spindle fibers built from the [[cytoskeleton]], which act like ropes that haul the chromosomes to their destinations.

Mitosis is usually followed by cytokinesis, the splitting of the cytoplasm that completes the formation of two separate cells. The result is two diploid cells, each with the same chromosome number as the parent. This stands in sharp contrast to [[meiosis]], the specialized division that creates reproductive cells with half the genetic material. Together, these two pathways represent the fundamental ways that eukaryotic cells reproduce, a topic explored further in [[how-cells-divide]].
