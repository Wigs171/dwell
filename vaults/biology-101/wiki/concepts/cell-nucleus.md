# Cell Nucleus

The **cell nucleus** is the largest and most prominent organelle in a typical [[eukaryotic-cell]], and it functions as the cell's control center. Its central job is to store and protect the cell's genetic information. Inside the nucleus lies the cell's [[dna]], which during most of the cell's life is loosely organized but condenses into distinct [[chromosome]] structures when the cell is preparing to divide. Because the genetic instructions for building every protein reside here, the nucleus effectively directs nearly everything the cell does.

The nucleus is wrapped in a double membrane called the **nuclear envelope**, which separates the genetic material from the rest of the cell. This envelope is not a complete seal; it is perforated by thousands of nuclear pores, protein-lined channels that regulate the passage of molecules in and out. Small molecules cross freely, while larger ones such as proteins and genetic messages move through the pores in a controlled way, allowing the nucleus to communicate with the cytoplasm without exposing its DNA to the cell's general activity.

One of the most important events that takes place inside the nucleus is [[transcription]], the process in which a stretch of DNA is copied into a working RNA message. That message then leaves through the nuclear pores to be used elsewhere in the cell. By keeping transcription physically separated from the rest of cellular life, the nucleus gives eukaryotic cells a layer of control over their genes that simpler cells lack.
