# Transcription

**Transcription** is the process of copying the information in a [[gene]] into a strand of messenger RNA. It is the first stage of gene expression, the path by which the instructions stored in [[dna]] are put to work. During transcription, only one of the two DNA strands, called the template strand, is read, and the cell produces a single-stranded molecule of [[rna]] that carries the same message in a slightly different chemical alphabet.

The work is done by an enzyme called RNA polymerase. It first binds to a special sequence near the start of a gene, known as a promoter, which marks where reading should begin. The enzyme then unwinds a short stretch of the double helix and moves along the template strand, adding RNA nucleotides one by one according to complementary base pairing. Wherever the template shows an adenine, the RNA receives uracil rather than thymine; guanine pairs with cytosine as usual. As RNA polymerase advances, the new messenger peels away and the DNA closes back into its double helix behind it.

Transcription ends when the enzyme reaches a stop signal called a terminator, releasing the finished messenger RNA. In eukaryotic cells this transcript is usually processed and edited before it leaves the nucleus. The completed messenger then carries the gene's instructions to the protein-building machinery, where it directs [[translation]]. Together, transcription and translation form the central flow of genetic information described in [[from-dna-to-protein]], turning a stable archive of DNA into the working molecules of life.
