# Ribosome

The **ribosome** is the molecular machine that builds proteins, making it one of the most essential structures in every living cell. It carries out [[translation]], the step in which the genetic instructions copied into messenger [[rna]] are read and used to link amino acids together in the correct order. Without ribosomes, the information stored in genes could never be turned into the working molecules that run a cell, so they are found in every organism, from the simplest bacterium to the largest plant or animal.

Structurally, a ribosome is made of two interlocking subunits, a large one and a small one, each built from ribosomal RNA combined with many proteins. This rRNA does more than hold the structure together; it actually performs the chemistry of joining amino acids, which makes the ribosome a kind of RNA enzyme. As a messenger RNA strand threads between the subunits, the ribosome moves along it three nucleotides at a time, matching each codon to the appropriate amino acid and stitching the growing chain into a finished [[protein]].

Cells position ribosomes in two ways. Free ribosomes float in the cytoplasm and typically make proteins used within the cell, while others attach to the rough endoplasmic reticulum, part of the [[endomembrane-system]], where they produce proteins destined for membranes, secretion, or specific organelles. This division of labor lets the cell direct each new protein toward its proper destination.
