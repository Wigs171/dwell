# Natural Selection

**Natural selection** is the central mechanism that explains how populations of living things become adapted to their environments. The idea rests on a few simple observations that combine into a powerful conclusion. First, individuals within a species vary in their traits. Second, much of this variation is heritable, meaning it can be passed from parent to offspring through the [[gene]]. Third, organisms tend to produce more offspring than the environment can support, so not all survive. When some inherited traits help their bearers survive and reproduce more successfully than others, those traits become more common in the next generation.

The measure of this reproductive success is called fitness, which in biology refers not to physical strength but to the number of surviving offspring an individual contributes to future generations. Over many generations, the steady accumulation of advantageous traits produces adaptation, the close fit between an organism and its way of life. A classic illustration is the way a population of insects can become resistant to a pesticide: the few individuals that happen to carry resistance survive, reproduce, and gradually come to dominate the population.

This mechanism was independently conceived in the nineteenth century by [[charles-darwin]], who recognized that selection acting on natural variation could, over immense spans of time, account for the diversity and design of living things without any guiding hand. Natural selection is therefore the primary driving force behind [[evolution]], the broader pattern of change in populations over time. Because it ties heredity, variation, and environment together into a single explanatory framework, it stands at the heart of [[why-evolution-unifies-biology]].
