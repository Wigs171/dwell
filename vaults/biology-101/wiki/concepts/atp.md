# ATP

**ATP**, short for adenosine triphosphate, is the molecule cells use as their main energy currency. Almost every energy-requiring task a cell performs, from contracting a muscle to pumping ions across a membrane to building large molecules, is paid for in ATP. Just as money lets you exchange value across many different transactions, ATP lets a cell move energy from the reactions that release it to the processes that need it, making it the universal go-between of cellular metabolism.

The molecule consists of an adenosine unit attached to a chain of three phosphate groups. The energy is stored in the bonds linking those phosphates, which are unstable because the negatively charged groups repel one another. When the cell needs energy, an [[enzyme]] breaks off the terminal phosphate, releasing usable energy and leaving behind ADP, a lower-energy form. That ADP is then recharged back into ATP, so the molecule is constantly recycled rather than consumed once and discarded; a typical cell turns over its entire ATP supply many times per minute.

ATP is regenerated by the cell's energy-harvesting pathways. In animals and most other organisms it is produced mainly through [[cellular-respiration]], which extracts energy from food using oxygen, with the bulk of it made in the [[mitochondrion]]. In plants and algae, [[photosynthesis]] also produces ATP, using sunlight as the original energy source. Either way, the cell's life depends on a continuous cycle of charging and spending this single, remarkable molecule.
