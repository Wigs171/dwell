# RNA

**RNA**, or ribonucleic acid, is a nucleic acid closely related to [[dna]] but suited to a more active, working role in the cell. Like DNA, it is built from nucleotides, but it differs in three important ways: its sugar is ribose rather than deoxyribose, it is usually single-stranded rather than a double helix, and it uses the base uracil in place of thymine. Uracil pairs with adenine just as thymine does, so RNA can still read and copy genetic information through complementary base pairing.

Cells make several kinds of RNA, each with a distinct job. Messenger RNA, or mRNA, is a working copy of a gene produced during [[transcription]]; it carries the coded instructions out of the nucleus to the protein-building machinery. Transfer RNA, or tRNA, acts as an adapter, matching each three-letter code on the messenger to its corresponding amino acid. Ribosomal RNA, or rRNA, is a structural and catalytic component of the [[ribosome]], the machine that assembles proteins. Together these forms make protein synthesis possible.

Because mRNA is single-stranded and short-lived, a cell can adjust how much of any protein it makes simply by controlling how much messenger it produces and how long that messenger survives. This flexibility lets the same genome express different proteins at different times. The final step, in which the message is decoded into a chain of amino acids, is called [[translation]]. RNA's central place in turning genetic instructions into living chemistry makes it one of the most important molecules in all of biology.
