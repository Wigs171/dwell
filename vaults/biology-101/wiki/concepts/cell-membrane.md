# Cell Membrane

Every cell is enclosed by a **cell membrane**, the thin boundary that separates the living interior from the outside world. Its core structure is a double layer of phospholipid molecules. Each phospholipid has a water-loving head and two water-fearing tails, so in a watery environment the molecules spontaneously arrange themselves into a bilayer with the tails tucked inward and the heads facing the water on both sides. This arrangement forms a stable, flexible sheet that is the foundation of the membrane.

Biologists describe the membrane with the **fluid mosaic model**. The word "fluid" captures the fact that the phospholipids and many of the embedded molecules drift sideways within the layer, giving the membrane a liquid-like quality. The word "mosaic" refers to the patchwork of [[protein]] molecules scattered throughout the bilayer. These membrane proteins do much of the real work, acting as channels and pumps, as receptors that receive signals, and as enzymes that speed up reactions at the surface.

A crucial property of this structure is selective permeability: the membrane lets some substances cross freely while blocking others. Small nonpolar molecules slip through the lipid layer easily by [[diffusion-and-osmosis]], whereas ions and large molecules need specific proteins to pass. By controlling this traffic, the membrane allows the cell to maintain an internal environment different from its surroundings, contributing directly to the [[homeostasis]] that keeps the cell alive. This same membrane design encloses the organelles inside every [[eukaryotic-cell]].
