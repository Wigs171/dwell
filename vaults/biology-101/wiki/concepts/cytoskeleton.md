# Cytoskeleton

The **cytoskeleton** is a dynamic network of protein fibers that runs throughout the cytoplasm of a [[eukaryotic-cell]]. Despite the name, it is not a rigid skeleton like ours; it constantly assembles and disassembles as the cell's needs change. It gives the cell its shape, anchors its organelles in place, provides tracks for internal transport, and generates the forces that let cells move and divide. Without it, a cell would be little more than a shapeless bag of fluid.

The cytoskeleton is built from three kinds of fibers, each made from different proteins and suited to different jobs. **Microfilaments**, the thinnest fibers, are made of the protein actin and are especially important for cell shape, for crawling movements, and for pinching a cell in two at the end of division. Intermediate filaments are tougher, rope-like fibers that resist mechanical stress and give cells durable structural strength. Microtubules, the thickest fibers, are hollow tubes of the protein tubulin that serve as highways for moving organelles and vesicles around the cell.

The cytoskeleton is also essential to cell division. During [[mitosis]], microtubules organize into a structure called the spindle, which attaches to the chromosomes and pulls the duplicated copies to opposite ends of the cell so that each new cell receives a complete set. In this way the same fibers that hold a resting cell together are repurposed to ensure that genetic information is shared accurately when the cell reproduces.
