# Chromosome

A **chromosome** is a single, very long molecule of [[dna]] packaged together with proteins into a compact, organized structure. A human chromosome stretched out would be far longer than the cell that holds it, so the DNA must be wound and folded to fit. It first coils around spool-like proteins called histones, forming a beaded fiber known as chromatin, which is then looped and condensed further. This packaging not only saves space but also protects the DNA and helps control which [[gene|genes]] are accessible at any given moment.

Most human cells contain 46 chromosomes, arranged as 23 matching pairs. One member of each pair comes from each parent, so every person inherits two copies of nearly every gene. Twenty-two of these pairs are alike in males and females, while the remaining pair, the sex chromosomes, differs and helps determine biological sex. Reproductive cells are an exception, carrying only a single set of 23.

Chromosomes become most visible when a cell prepares to divide, because the chromatin condenses tightly so the genetic material can be moved without tangling. During ordinary cell division, or [[mitosis]], each chromosome is first duplicated and then split evenly so that both daughter cells receive a complete and identical set. During [[meiosis]], the special division that makes reproductive cells, the chromosome number is halved so that fertilization can restore the full count. In this way, chromosomes act as the cell's carefully managed filing system for its entire genetic library.
