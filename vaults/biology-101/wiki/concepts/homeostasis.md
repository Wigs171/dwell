# Homeostasis

**Homeostasis** is the ability of a living system to maintain relatively stable internal conditions even as the outside world changes. Cells and bodies function properly only within narrow ranges of temperature, acidity, water content, and the concentrations of dissolved substances. If these conditions drift too far, vital processes fail. Homeostasis is therefore not a state of rigid constancy but a dynamic balance, continuously adjusted to keep the internal environment within tolerable limits.

The mechanism behind this balance is feedback. In negative feedback, the most common form, a change in some condition triggers a response that counteracts the change and restores the original state. The regulation of body temperature is a familiar example: when you become too warm, sweating and dilated blood vessels release heat, while when you become too cold, shivering generates it. Similar loops govern the pH of body fluids and the balance of water and salts. Much of this water balance is managed at the boundary of every cell, where the [[cell-membrane]] controls what enters and leaves, and where the physics of [[diffusion-and-osmosis]] determines how water moves across that barrier.

Homeostasis matters so much in part because the chemistry of life is delicate. Each [[enzyme]] that speeds a reaction works best within a specific range of temperature and acidity, and outside that range it slows or stops. By holding internal conditions steady, an organism keeps its thousands of enzyme-driven reactions running smoothly. In this sense, homeostasis is the quiet, constant labor that allows all the other processes of life to proceed reliably from one moment to the next.
