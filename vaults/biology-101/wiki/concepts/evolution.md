# Evolution

**Evolution** is the change in the heritable characteristics of populations over successive generations. It is important to note that individuals do not evolve; populations do. Over time, the frequencies of different versions of a [[gene]] shift within a group of organisms, and as these shifts accumulate, the population gradually comes to differ from its ancestors. This continuous transformation is what biologists mean by descent with modification, the phrase that captures how living things inherit features from their forebears while also acquiring changes.

A central insight of evolutionary biology is common ancestry. Because all living things share the same genetic code and many of the same fundamental molecular machines, the evidence points to a single tree of life in which every species traces back to shared ancestors. The branching pattern of this tree explains why organisms that seem very different, such as a whale and a bat, nonetheless reveal deep similarities in their underlying structure. Comparative anatomy, the fossil record, and molecular sequences all converge on the same story of relatedness.

Evolution is driven by several processes, but the most important for producing adaptation is [[natural-selection]], which sorts heritable variation according to survival and reproduction. The unifying power of this idea was first articulated by [[charles-darwin]], whose work transformed biology from a catalog of separate facts into a coherent science. Indeed, evolution provides the framework that connects genetics, anatomy, ecology, and behavior into one explanatory whole, the central argument of [[why-evolution-unifies-biology]].
