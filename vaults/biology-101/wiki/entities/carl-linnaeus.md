# Carl Linnaeus

**Carl Linnaeus** (1707–1778) was a Swedish botanist, physician, and naturalist who is regarded as the father of modern taxonomy, the science of naming and classifying living things. At a time when descriptions of plants and animals were often long, inconsistent Latin phrases, Linnaeus sought a clear and orderly system that scientists everywhere could share and understand.

His most enduring contribution was binomial nomenclature, a method of giving every species a standardized two-part Latin name consisting of a genus name followed by a species name, such as Homo sapiens for human beings. He set out this system in his influential work *Systema Naturae*, first published in 1735 and expanded over many later editions, and applied it systematically to thousands of plants and animals. Linnaeus also organized organisms into a nested hierarchy of ranks, grouping species into broader and broader categories, which established the framework still used to show [[how-life-is-organized]].

Linnaeus worked before the theory of common descent, and he viewed his categories as a way to reveal the order of nature rather than as a record of ancestry. Yet his hierarchical groupings, by clustering similar organisms together, later proved deeply compatible with [[evolution]], since species that share a recent common ancestor tend to resemble one another. His naming system remains the universal language of biology today.
