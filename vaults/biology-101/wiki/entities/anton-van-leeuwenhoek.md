# Anton van Leeuwenhoek

**Anton van Leeuwenhoek** (1632–1723) was a Dutch tradesman and self-taught scientist from the city of Delft who became one of the founders of microbiology. Trained as a draper rather than a scholar, he taught himself to grind tiny, exceptionally clear glass lenses and used them to build simple single-lens microscopes that magnified far better than the compound instruments of his day. With these devices he opened a window onto a previously invisible living world.

Beginning in the 1670s, Leeuwenhoek examined samples of pond water, rainwater, saliva, and scrapings from his own teeth. To his astonishment, he saw countless tiny creatures swimming and tumbling within the droplets, which he described as "animalcules," meaning little animals. He was the first person to observe bacteria, single-celled protists, and other microorganisms, and he reported his findings in a long series of letters to the Royal Society in London. Many of the organisms he described were the kind built from a [[prokaryotic-cell]], the simplest form of life on Earth.

His meticulous observations provided some of the earliest direct evidence that life exists at the microscopic scale, profoundly expanding the boundaries of biology. Together with the structural insights of earlier microscopists, Leeuwenhoek's discoveries helped pave the way for the [[cell-theory]] and for the modern understanding that living things are composed of cells.
