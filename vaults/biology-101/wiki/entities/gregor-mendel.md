# Gregor Mendel

**Gregor Mendel** (1822–1884) was an Austrian friar and naturalist who lived and worked at the Augustinian monastery in Brno, in what is now the Czech Republic. Combining a monastic life with a serious interest in mathematics and natural science, he carried out a remarkable series of breeding experiments in the monastery garden during the 1850s and 1860s, using the common garden pea as his subject.

By carefully crossing pea plants that differed in single, clearly distinguishable traits such as seed color, seed shape, and flower position, and by counting thousands of offspring, Mendel detected consistent numerical patterns in how traits were passed from one generation to the next. He concluded that inheritance is governed by discrete factors, now understood to be units called the [[gene]], that come in pairs and separate during the formation of reproductive cells. From this work he derived his principles of segregation and independent assortment, which together describe how parental traits are shuffled and combined in offspring.

Mendel published his findings in 1866, but their significance went largely unrecognized until around 1900, after his death. Today his factors are known to reside on the [[chromosome]], and the separation he inferred is explained by the cell division called [[meiosis]]. His quantitative approach earned him lasting recognition as the founder of modern genetics.
