# How Cells Divide

A single fertilized egg becomes a body of trillions of cells, and a wound heals as new cells replace lost ones, yet sexual reproduction also demands cells that are deliberately unlike their parents. To meet these contrasting needs, life uses two distinct forms of cell division. Both begin the same way but end with very different results, and the comparison reveals how organisms balance faithful copying against the variation that evolution requires.

Every division must first solve the same problem: the genetic instructions have to be duplicated so each daughter cell receives a complete set. This is handled by [[dna-replication]], which copies each molecule of [[dna]] before division begins. The copied material is then organized into compact [[chromosome]] structures, with each original chromosome now paired to an identical sister copy, ready to be sorted into the new cells.

The more common route is [[mitosis]], the division behind growth, tissue repair, and the everyday replacement of worn-out cells. Here the sister copies of every chromosome are separated and pulled to opposite ends of the cell, so that when the cell splits, each daughter receives a full and identical chromosome set. The two new cells are genetic carbon copies of the original, which is exactly what a body needs when building or mending tissue.

The other route is [[meiosis]], reserved for making the gametes, eggs and sperm, used in sexual reproduction. It involves two rounds of division rather than one, halving the chromosome number so that two gametes can later combine without doubling it. Along the way, matching chromosomes exchange segments and assort independently, shuffling the parental genes into fresh combinations. The outcome is four cells, each genetically unique and carrying half the usual chromosomes. Together these two processes let life both copy itself perfectly and reinvent itself endlessly.
