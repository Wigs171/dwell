# Prokaryotes vs. Eukaryotes

All cellular life falls into two great structural categories, and the difference between them is one of the most fundamental divisions in biology. On one side sits the [[prokaryotic-cell]], the form found in bacteria and archaea; on the other sits the [[eukaryotic-cell]], the form that builds protists, fungi, plants, and animals. Understanding what separates them clarifies how cells evolved from simple beginnings into the elaborate compartments we see today.

The defining contrast is the handling of genetic material. A prokaryotic cell has no [[cell-nucleus]]; its DNA floats freely in the cytoplasm in a region called the nucleoid. A eukaryotic cell, by contrast, seals its DNA inside a membrane-bound nucleus, separating the storage and reading of genes from the rest of the cell's chemistry. This single architectural choice opens the door to much greater regulation and complexity.

The two types also differ in compartmentalization and scale. Prokaryotes are small and largely undivided inside, carrying out their reactions in a shared interior space. Eukaryotes are typically far larger and partition their work among membrane-bound organelles, each a specialized workshop. Among these are the [[mitochondrion]], which produces energy, and in photosynthetic cells the [[chloroplast]], which captures light. This division of labor lets eukaryotic cells run many incompatible processes at once.

Where did those energy-producing organelles come from? The endosymbiotic theory, championed by [[lynn-margulis]], proposes that they were once free-living prokaryotes engulfed by a larger host cell. Rather than being digested, the captured cells survived and became permanent partners, eventually evolving into organelles. The evidence is striking: mitochondria and chloroplasts have their own small genomes, divide on their own, and are wrapped in double membranes. In this view, the complex eukaryotic cell is itself a community of formerly independent prokaryotes that learned to live as one.
