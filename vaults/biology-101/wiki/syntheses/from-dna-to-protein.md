# From DNA to Protein

A cell's most precious possession is information, and biology stores that information as a sequence of chemical letters along a molecule of [[dna]]. The central dogma of molecular biology describes how this stored information is read out and turned into the working machinery of the cell. Information flows in one main direction, from DNA to an intermediate copy and finally to a finished [[protein]], the molecule that actually does the work.

The unit of that information is the [[gene]], a stretch of DNA that specifies one product, usually a single protein. Because the precious master copy stays safely archived, the cell does not use it directly. Instead, the first step is [[transcription]], in which an enzyme reads one strand of the gene and synthesizes a matching strand of [[rna]]. This messenger RNA is a short, disposable transcript that carries the gene's instructions out to where they can be acted upon, leaving the original DNA intact.

The second step is [[translation]], where the language of nucleotides is converted into the language of amino acids. This happens at the [[ribosome]], a molecular machine that clamps onto the messenger RNA and reads it three letters at a time. Each three-letter code calls for a particular amino acid, which is delivered by transfer RNA and stitched onto a growing chain. As the ribosome travels the length of the message, it assembles amino acids in the exact order the gene specified.

The finished chain then folds into a precise three-dimensional shape, and that shape determines what the protein can do, whether acting as an [[enzyme]], a structural fiber, or a signal. In this way a one-dimensional string of letters becomes a functioning three-dimensional tool. The same flow operates in every organism, which is why the central dogma is one of the deepest unifying ideas in all of biology.
