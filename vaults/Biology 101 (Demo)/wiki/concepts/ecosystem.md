# Ecosystem

An **ecosystem** is the combination of all the living organisms in a particular area and the nonliving physical environment with which they interact. It includes the plants, animals, fungi, and microbes of a place along with the sunlight, water, air, soil, and minerals that surround them. A forest, a coral reef, a pond, and even a patch of soil can each be studied as an ecosystem, because in each the living and nonliving parts are bound together by a constant exchange of energy and materials.

Two great patterns organize how ecosystems work. The first is the flow of energy. Energy enters most ecosystems as sunlight, which producers such as green plants capture through [[photosynthesis]] and store in chemical form. That energy then passes to the organisms that eat the plants, and onward to those that eat the plant-eaters, growing scarcer at each step as some is lost as heat. The second pattern is the cycling of matter. Unlike energy, which flows through and dissipates, chemical elements such as carbon and nitrogen are used again and again, released by decomposers and returned to the soil, water, and air to be taken up once more.

These flows and cycles connect organisms into food webs and shape the whole structure of an ecosystem. The traits that fit each species to its particular role are themselves products of [[evolution]], acting over long stretches of time. Understanding ecosystems also requires seeing where they sit within the broader nested scales of life, from molecules and cells up to the entire biosphere, a perspective developed in [[how-life-is-organized]].
