# Endomembrane System

The **endomembrane system** is a coordinated group of membrane-bound organelles within a [[eukaryotic-cell]] that work together like an assembly line to produce, process, and distribute the cell's molecules. Its members are physically connected, either directly or by small transport sacs called vesicles that bud off one compartment and fuse with another. The result is a continuous internal manufacturing and shipping network that handles much of the cell's chemistry.

The system begins with the **endoplasmic reticulum**, an extensive set of folded membranes that extends from the nuclear envelope. The rough endoplasmic reticulum is studded with [[ribosome]] particles and folds and processes newly made proteins as they emerge, while the smooth endoplasmic reticulum lacks ribosomes and instead synthesizes lipids and helps detoxify various substances. Proteins and lipids leave the reticulum inside vesicles bound for the next station.

That next station is the Golgi apparatus, a stack of flattened membranes that acts as the cell's finishing and sorting center. Here molecules are chemically modified, tagged, and packaged into new vesicles addressed to their final destinations. Some vesicles deliver their contents to the [[cell-membrane]] for secretion, while others become lysosomes, organelles filled with digestive enzymes that break down worn-out parts and engulfed material. Through this orderly sequence, the endomembrane system ensures that each [[protein]] the cell builds is correctly folded, packaged, and sent where it is needed.
