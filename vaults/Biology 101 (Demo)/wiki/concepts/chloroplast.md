# Chloroplast

The **chloroplast** is the organelle in plant and algal cells responsible for [[photosynthesis]], the process that captures light energy and uses it to build sugars. Found only in [[eukaryotic-cell|eukaryotic cells]] that are capable of harvesting sunlight, chloroplasts are what make plants the foundation of most food chains, since they convert simple inorganic ingredients into the energy-rich molecules that nearly all other organisms ultimately depend on.

Like the mitochondrion, the chloroplast is bounded by a double membrane, but inside it contains a third membrane system folded into flattened sacs called **thylakoids**. Stacks of these thylakoids form structures called grana, and their membranes are studded with chlorophyll, the green pigment that absorbs light and gives plants their color. The light-driven reactions occur on the thylakoid membranes, where light energy is used to generate [[atp]] and another energy carrier, while the surrounding fluid, called the stroma, hosts the reactions that assemble sugar from carbon dioxide.

Chloroplasts share the same hallmark of independence seen in mitochondria: they carry their own circular DNA and ribosomes and can synthesize some of their own proteins. This evidence supports the endosymbiotic theory developed by [[lynn-margulis]], which holds that chloroplasts originated from free-living photosynthetic bacteria, similar to today's cyanobacteria, that were taken up by an early host cell. That ancient merger gave rise to the photosynthetic lineages that now oxygenate the atmosphere and feed the living world.
