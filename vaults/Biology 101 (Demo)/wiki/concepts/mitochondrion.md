# Mitochondrion

The **mitochondrion** is a double-membraned organelle found in nearly all [[eukaryotic-cell|eukaryotic cells]], where it serves as the primary site of aerobic energy production. Often called the powerhouse of the cell, it is where the later stages of [[cellular-respiration]] take place, converting the chemical energy in nutrients into [[atp]], the molecule cells use to power their work. A cell's mitochondria number from one to many thousands, with the count roughly matching how much energy a given tissue demands; muscle and heart cells, for example, are densely packed with them.

Its structure is closely tied to its job. The outer membrane is smooth and permeable to small molecules, while the inner membrane folds into deep ridges called **cristae** that vastly increase its surface area. These folds hold the electron transport chain and the enzymes that synthesize ATP, so a larger inner surface means more capacity for energy production. The space enclosed by the inner membrane, the matrix, contains the enzymes of the citric acid cycle.

Mitochondria are unusual among organelles because they carry their own small loop of DNA and their own ribosomes, allowing them to make some of their proteins independently. This semi-autonomy is the central evidence for the endosymbiotic theory, championed by [[lynn-margulis]], which proposes that mitochondria descend from free-living bacteria engulfed by an ancestral host cell. That ancient partnership reshaped the history of complex life, giving eukaryotes an efficient way to extract energy from food using oxygen.
