# Prokaryotic Cell

A **prokaryotic cell** is the simpler and more ancient of the two basic cell designs found on Earth. The word means "before the nucleus," and indeed the defining feature of these cells is that they have no true nucleus and no membrane-bound organelles. The two great domains of life that are built from prokaryotic cells are the bacteria and the archaea, organisms that are almost always single-celled and usually only a few micrometers across.

Even without internal compartments, a prokaryotic cell is a fully functioning living thing. Its genetic material, a single circular molecule of [[dna]], floats in a region of the cytoplasm called the **nucleoid**, which is not separated from the rest of the cell by any membrane. Surrounding the cytoplasm is a [[cell-membrane]] that controls what enters and leaves, and outside that most prokaryotes have a protective cell wall. Scattered through the cytoplasm are large numbers of [[ribosome]] particles, the molecular machines that build proteins.

What prokaryotes lack in internal structure they make up for in metabolic variety and sheer numbers. They were the only life on the planet for billions of years and remain by far the most abundant organisms today, living in soil, oceans, and even inside our own bodies. The contrast between this streamlined plan and the compartmentalized [[eukaryotic-cell]] is one of the most important distinctions in all of biology, a theme explored further in [[prokaryotes-vs-eukaryotes]].
