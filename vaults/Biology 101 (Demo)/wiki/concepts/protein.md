# Protein

A **protein** is a large molecule built from a chain of smaller units called amino acids. There are twenty common amino acids, and they can be linked in any order and length, so the number of possible proteins is enormous. The chain is assembled during [[translation]], when a [[ribosome]] reads a genetic message and joins amino acids together one by one in the sequence specified by a [[gene]]. This sequence is the protein's most basic level of organization and determines everything that follows.

What makes a protein useful is not the chain itself but the shape it folds into. As the chain is built, attractions and repulsions between its amino acids cause it to twist, coil, and fold into a precise three-dimensional structure. Because that shape arises from the sequence, a single change in the order of amino acids can alter the fold and change how the protein behaves. The finished shape creates surfaces and pockets that allow the protein to do its specific job.

Proteins carry out most of the work of a living cell. Many are [[enzyme|enzymes]] that speed up chemical reactions, allowing processes that would otherwise be far too slow to support life. Others provide structure, forming fibers and frameworks that give cells and tissues their strength and form. Still others act in signaling and transport, carrying messages between cells, moving substances across membranes, or defending the body against invaders. Because they translate genetic instructions into action, proteins are often described as the molecular machines that bring the genome to life.
