# Glycolysis

**Glycolysis** is the first stage of breaking down sugar for energy, a sequence of reactions that splits a single molecule of glucose into two smaller molecules called pyruvate. Its name literally means the splitting of sugar, and it takes place in the cytoplasm rather than inside any organelle. Glycolysis is one of the most ancient and universal of all metabolic pathways, occurring in nearly every living cell, which suggests it arose very early in the history of life, before cells had specialized energy organelles.

The pathway proceeds in about ten enzyme-catalyzed steps. At first the cell actually invests energy, using two molecules of [[atp]] to prime the glucose and make it reactive. The six-carbon sugar is then cleaved into two three-carbon fragments, which are processed further in a series of steps that release energy. By the end, the pathway produces four ATP and captures additional energy on electron carriers, giving a small net gain of two ATP per glucose. Importantly, glycolysis requires no oxygen, so it can run under anaerobic conditions.

Because of this independence from oxygen, glycolysis serves as the shared starting point for several energy strategies. When oxygen is available, the pyruvate it produces is passed into the [[mitochondrion]] to be fully oxidized by [[cellular-respiration]], unlocking far more energy. When oxygen is absent, the cell instead routes pyruvate into fermentation, sacrificing yield but keeping glycolysis running so that at least a modest, steady supply of ATP continues.
