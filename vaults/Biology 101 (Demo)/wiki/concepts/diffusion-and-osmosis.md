# Diffusion and Osmosis

**Diffusion** is the tendency of molecules to spread out from where they are crowded to where they are sparse. Because particles are in constant random motion, they naturally drift from a region of high concentration toward a region of low concentration until they are evenly distributed. This movement requires no energy input from the cell, which is why it is called passive transport. The difference in concentration that drives the process is known as a concentration gradient, and diffusion always proceeds "down" that gradient.

When the diffusing substance is water moving across a [[cell-membrane]], the process gets a special name: **osmosis**. Cell membranes are selectively permeable, so water can pass but many dissolved substances cannot. Water therefore moves from the side where it is more abundant, meaning the side with fewer dissolved particles, to the side where it is less abundant, meaning the side with more dissolved particles. In effect, water flows toward the more concentrated solution.

The direction of this water movement is summarized by the idea of tonicity. In a hypotonic surrounding solution, water rushes into the cell and it may swell or burst; in a hypertonic solution, water leaves and the cell shrinks; in an isotonic solution, water moves in and out at equal rates and the cell holds its shape. Managing these water movements is essential to a cell's survival, making osmosis a key part of how an organism maintains [[homeostasis]] and balances its internal fluids.
