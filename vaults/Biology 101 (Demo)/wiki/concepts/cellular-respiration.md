# Cellular Respiration

**Cellular respiration** is the process by which cells extract energy from food, most often the sugar glucose, and capture it in the form of [[atp]]. In its complete aerobic form, respiration uses oxygen to break glucose all the way down to carbon dioxide and water, releasing a large amount of usable energy along the way. This is how most organisms, including humans, power their cells, and it is essentially the reverse of [[photosynthesis]], consuming the sugar and oxygen that photosynthetic organisms produce.

The process unfolds in three connected stages. It begins with [[glycolysis]], which takes place in the cytoplasm and splits glucose into two molecules of pyruvate, yielding a small net amount of ATP without requiring oxygen. The pyruvate then enters the [[mitochondrion]], where the citric acid cycle, also called the Krebs cycle, finishes dismantling the carbon skeletons and loads energy onto electron-carrier molecules. Finally, those carriers feed the electron transport chain on the inner mitochondrial membrane, where the flow of electrons toward oxygen drives the production of the great majority of the cell's ATP.

Overall, the complete oxidation of one glucose molecule can yield roughly thirty or so ATP, far more than glycolysis alone provides, which is why aerobic respiration is so efficient. When oxygen is scarce, cells fall back on fermentation, accepting much lower energy yields. Understanding how these stages fit together is the heart of [[how-cells-make-energy]], one of the unifying themes of cell biology.
