# DNA

**DNA**, or deoxyribonucleic acid, is the molecule that carries the hereditary instructions of nearly every living thing. Its structure is a double helix, two long strands wound around each other like a twisted ladder. Each strand is built from repeating units called nucleotides, and each nucleotide contains one of four nitrogenous bases: adenine, thymine, guanine, and cytosine, usually written as A, T, G, and C. The backbone of each strand is made of alternating sugar and phosphate groups, which gives the molecule its strength and direction.

The two strands are held together by a rule called complementary base pairing. Adenine always pairs with thymine, and guanine always pairs with cytosine, joined by hydrogen bonds. Because of this rule, the sequence of one strand automatically specifies the sequence of the other, which makes the molecule easy to copy faithfully. This property is the chemical foundation of [[dna-replication]], the process that duplicates the genetic material before a cell divides.

The order of bases along a strand is a code. A specific stretch of DNA that carries the instructions for a product is called a [[gene]], and a cell's full set of genes is distributed among its [[chromosome|chromosomes]], where the long DNA molecule is packed tightly with proteins. To put its information to use, a cell first copies a gene into a related molecule, [[rna]], which then guides the building of proteins. Our modern understanding of the double helix rests in part on the X-ray work of [[rosalind-franklin]], whose images revealed the molecule's helical shape.
