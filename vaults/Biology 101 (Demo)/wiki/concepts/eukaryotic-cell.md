# Eukaryotic Cell

A **eukaryotic cell** is the larger, more complex kind of cell, distinguished by the presence of a true nucleus and a collection of membrane-bound compartments called organelles. The name means "true nucleus," and that nucleus is the cell's most obvious internal feature. All animals, plants, fungi, and protists are built from eukaryotic cells, whether they exist as a single cell or as part of a body made of trillions.

The key advantage of this design is the division of labor among separate compartments. The [[cell-nucleus]] safely houses the cell's DNA and directs its activities. The [[mitochondrion]] supplies usable chemical energy by breaking down fuel molecules. The [[endomembrane-system]] manufactures, modifies, and ships proteins and lipids through a network of connected membranes. Holding everything in place and enabling movement is the [[cytoskeleton]], a scaffold of protein fibers running throughout the cell. Plant cells add a further organelle, the chloroplast, for capturing light energy.

Because each organelle can maintain its own chemical environment, a eukaryotic cell can carry out many incompatible reactions at once, something a simpler cell cannot easily do. This compartmentalization allowed eukaryotes to grow larger and to evolve into the multicellular organisms we see around us. The sharp difference between this plan and that of the streamlined [[prokaryotic-cell]] marks one of the deepest divides in the living world.
