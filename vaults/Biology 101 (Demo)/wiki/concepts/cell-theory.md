# Cell Theory

The **cell theory** is one of the great unifying ideas in biology. It holds three central claims: that every living organism is composed of one or more cells, that the cell is the smallest unit that can be called alive, and that all cells come from other cells already in existence. Taken together, these statements mean that no matter how different a bacterium, an oak tree, and a human being appear, they share the same basic building block and the same kind of origin.

The idea grew slowly out of the invention of the microscope in the seventeenth century. The English natural philosopher [[robert-hooke]] coined the word "cell" in 1665 after looking at thin slices of cork and noticing tiny box-like chambers that reminded him of the rooms in a monastery. Around the same period the Dutch lens-maker [[anton-van-leeuwenhoek]] ground his own remarkably powerful lenses and became the first person to observe living single-celled organisms, including bacteria and protozoa, in pond water and other samples.

It took nearly two more centuries for biologists to gather the observations needed to state the theory formally, recognizing that both the simple [[prokaryotic-cell]] and the more complex [[eukaryotic-cell]] obey the same rules. The third claim, that cells arise only from other cells, was especially important because it ruled out the older belief that living things could form spontaneously from nonliving matter. Cell theory therefore tied all life to a single shared chemistry and a continuous chain of cell division stretching back through time.
