# How Life Is Organized

Biology makes sense once you notice that life is built in nested layers, each level emerging from the one below it. At the smallest scale, atoms join into molecules, and certain large molecules such as proteins and nucleic acids carry the chemistry of life. These molecules assemble into the structures and machinery that fill a living cell, and at that point a remarkable threshold is crossed: the molecules become alive together in a way none of them is alone.

The cell is the pivotal level in this hierarchy, a fact captured by [[cell-theory]], which holds that all organisms are made of cells and that every cell comes from a preexisting cell. In complex organisms the workhorse unit is the [[eukaryotic-cell]], and many such cells of one type group into tissues, tissues combine into organs, and organs cooperate as organ systems. A whole organism is the sum of these coordinated systems working as one self-maintaining individual.

Above the level of the individual, organization continues outward. Organisms of the same species living in one area form a population, and the different populations sharing a place form a community. When that community is considered together with the soil, water, air, and energy flowing through it, we have an [[ecosystem]], the level at which living and nonliving components interact as a functioning whole. Larger groupings of ecosystems eventually compose the biosphere, the thin shell of life covering the planet.

To study this profusion of organisms, we need a way to name and sort them. The system still in use was devised by [[carl-linnaeus]], who gave every species a two-part Latin name and arranged living things into a ranked set of nested groups. His classification scheme mirrors the layered logic of life itself, grouping similar organisms within ever-broader categories. From a single molecule to the entire biosphere, biology is a story of parts assembling into wholes that then become parts of something larger.
