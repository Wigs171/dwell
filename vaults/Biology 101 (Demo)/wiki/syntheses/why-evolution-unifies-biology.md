# Why Evolution Unifies Biology

The geneticist Theodosius Dobzhansky famously declared that nothing in biology makes sense except in the light of evolution, and the claim is not an exaggeration. Without a single organizing idea, the living world would be a bewildering catalog of unrelated facts. With [[evolution]] as the frame, those facts snap into a coherent pattern, because the diversity of life turns out to be the branching record of a shared history.

The engine that drives much of this change is [[natural-selection]], the mechanism that [[charles-darwin]] described. Within any population, individuals vary, more offspring are produced than can survive, and those whose traits happen to suit the environment leave more descendants. Over many generations the helpful variations accumulate and populations gradually change, sometimes splitting into new species. The result is descent with modification, a tree of life in which every branch shares a common root.

The deepest evidence for that common ancestry lies in the molecules of the cell. All known organisms store their instructions in [[dna]], read in nearly the same genetic code, and many of the same [[gene]] sequences recur across creatures as different as bacteria, yeast, and humans. If life had arisen many separate times, there would be no reason for this shared machinery; the fact that it is universal points strongly to a single origin from which everything else descends.

This is why evolution is not just one topic among many but the thread that ties the whole subject together. It connects the chemistry of the cell to the anatomy of organisms, the distribution of species across continents, and the fossil record laid down over deep time. Every other field of biology, from molecular genetics to ecology, gains its explanatory power by asking how present forms came to be. Evolution supplies that answer, and in doing so it transforms a heap of observations into a single, comprehensible science.
