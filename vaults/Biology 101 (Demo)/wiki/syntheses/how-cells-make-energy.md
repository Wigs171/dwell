# How Cells Make Energy

Almost every activity inside a living cell costs energy, and life has settled on a single tradeable currency to pay for it. That currency is [[atp]], a small molecule whose chemical bonds can be assembled to bank energy and broken to spend it. The story of how cells make energy is really the story of how the energy in sunlight is captured, stored in stable chemical form, and later released in small, usable amounts.

The capturing step is [[photosynthesis]], carried out in the [[chloroplast]] of plants and algae. Pigments absorb light and drive reactions that combine carbon dioxide and water into sugar, releasing oxygen as a byproduct. In effect, the energy of photons is locked into the bonds of glucose, a dense and portable fuel that the organism can store or transport. Without this step, there would be no concentrated chemical energy for the rest of the living world to draw upon.

Releasing that stored energy is the job of [[cellular-respiration]]. It begins with [[glycolysis]] in the cytoplasm, where glucose is split into smaller molecules and a little ATP is produced directly. The real payoff comes inside the [[mitochondrion]], where those products are oxidized through the citric acid cycle and the electron transport chain, generating a large amount of ATP while consuming oxygen and producing carbon dioxide and water. Each transformation is steered by a specific [[enzyme]], a protein catalyst that lowers the energy barrier and lets the reaction proceed quickly at body temperature.

Seen together, the two processes form a loop. Photosynthesis pulls in carbon dioxide and water and emits oxygen and sugar; respiration runs the same chemistry in reverse, consuming sugar and oxygen to regenerate carbon dioxide and water. Light energy enters the loop, useful work and heat leave it, and ATP shuttles the energy across that gap moment by moment.
