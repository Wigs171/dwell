# Robert Hooke

**Robert Hooke** (1635–1703) was an English natural philosopher, inventor, and one of the most versatile experimenters of the seventeenth century. Working as Curator of Experiments for the newly founded Royal Society in London, he investigated subjects ranging from springs and gravitation to optics and astronomy. He is remembered in biology above all for his pioneering use of the compound microscope to reveal a hidden world of structure too small for the unaided eye to detect.

In 1665 Hooke published *Micrographia*, a lavishly illustrated volume containing his detailed engravings of insects, plant tissues, and other minute objects. When he examined a thin slice of cork, he saw that it was divided into tiny boxlike compartments, which reminded him of the small rooms, or cells, occupied by monks in a monastery. He named these compartments "cells," giving science a word that endures to this day. What Hooke actually observed were the empty walls of dead plant tissue rather than living matter, yet his observation was a crucial first step toward the [[cell-theory]] that would develop over the following two centuries.

Although Hooke could not have known that cells are the basic units of all living things, his careful drawings and terminology laid important groundwork. His work helped later researchers recognize that organisms, including those built from the more complex [[eukaryotic-cell]], are composed of these fundamental building blocks.
