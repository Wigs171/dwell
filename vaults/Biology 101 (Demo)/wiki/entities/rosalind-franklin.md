# Rosalind Franklin

**Rosalind Franklin** (1920–1958) was a British chemist and X-ray crystallographer whose precise experimental work was essential to one of the greatest discoveries in the history of biology. Trained in physical chemistry at the University of Cambridge, she became an expert in X-ray diffraction, a technique in which beams of X-rays are passed through a substance and the resulting scattering pattern is used to deduce the arrangement of its atoms.

While working at King's College London in the early 1950s, Franklin and her student produced exceptionally clear diffraction images of [[dna]] fibers. The most famous of these, known as "Photo 51," revealed a distinctive X-shaped pattern that indicated a helical structure and provided key measurements of its dimensions and spacing. Her data showed that the molecule was a double helix with the sugar-phosphate backbones on the outside, crucial details that constrained any correct model of the molecule.

In 1953 James Watson and Francis Crick used insights drawn from Franklin's images and measurements, along with other evidence, to build their celebrated model of the double helix. Franklin died of cancer in 1958 at the age of thirty-seven, before the 1962 Nobel Prize was awarded for the discovery, and for decades her central contribution was underappreciated. She is now widely honored as a key figure in revealing the molecular basis of heredity.
