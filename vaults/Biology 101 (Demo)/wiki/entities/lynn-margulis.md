# Lynn Margulis

**Lynn Margulis** (1938–2011) was an American evolutionary biologist whose bold ideas reshaped scientists' understanding of how complex cells came to be. Working in the United States during the 1960s and beyond, she revived and powerfully argued for a once-dismissed idea: that some of the structures inside complex cells were originally independent bacteria that took up residence within a host cell long ago.

Her endosymbiotic theory proposed that the [[mitochondrion]], the organelle that supplies most of a cell's usable energy, descended from free-living bacteria capable of using oxygen, and that the [[chloroplast]], the organelle that carries out photosynthesis in plants and algae, descended from photosynthetic bacteria. Over evolutionary time these engulfed microbes became permanent partners, and their descendants now function as essential parts of the larger cell. Margulis pointed to compelling evidence, including the fact that these organelles have their own DNA and reproduce by dividing, much as bacteria do.

Initially her proposal met strong resistance, but it gradually won wide acceptance and is now a cornerstone of cell biology. The theory explains a key step in the rise of the [[eukaryotic-cell]], the kind of complex, compartmentalized cell that makes up plants, animals, fungi, and protists. Margulis's emphasis on cooperation and symbiosis as creative forces in evolution remains highly influential.
