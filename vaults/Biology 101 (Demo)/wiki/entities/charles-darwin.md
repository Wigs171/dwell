# Charles Darwin

**Charles Darwin** (1809–1882) was an English naturalist and geologist whose work transformed the way humanity understands the living world. As a young man he sailed aboard HMS Beagle on a nearly five-year voyage around the globe, during which he collected specimens and made observations of plants, animals, and fossils across South America and on the Galápagos Islands. The remarkable variation he noticed among species on different islands, and the relationships between living creatures and extinct ones, set him on a decades-long search for an explanation.

Darwin proposed that organisms vary in heritable traits, that more individuals are born than can survive, and that those with advantageous traits tend to leave more offspring. Over many generations this process gradually changes populations, a mechanism he called [[natural-selection]]. In 1859 he published *On the Origin of Species*, presenting a vast body of evidence and arguing that all living things share common ancestry. His ideas provided a unifying explanation for the diversity and adaptation seen throughout nature.

Although Darwin did not know how traits were inherited, his theory of [[evolution]] became the central organizing principle of modern biology. The way his insight connects anatomy, genetics, ecology, and the history of life is explored further in [[why-evolution-unifies-biology]].
