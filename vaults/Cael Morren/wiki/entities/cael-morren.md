# Cael Morren

## The City Built to Be Drowned

Cael Morren stands in tiers against a sea that keeps no daily promise. It answers instead to [[the-long-tide|the Long Tide]], a rise and fall spread across roughly forty years, so that a person may be born in a dry street and die watching fish move through its window. The city was raised knowing this. Its lower storeys are made plain and sacrificial — thick stone, wide doors, nothing loved left below the mark — because when the flood comes they will be given to the water without grief. The people simply move up. Bakeries become boat-sheds; a magistrate's hall becomes a mooring. Nothing here is meant to last, and so, strangely, everything endures.

## Rhythm of Drowning and Return

Life follows the water like a slow tide-clock. At full flood the sea climbs to the second storeys and the streets become canals of green light and floating tallow-lamps. At the low ebb the sea withdraws and [[the-undercity]] surfaces — the drowned older town, dripping and barnacled, walkable for a single season before the water takes it back. Between these poles the city breathes. Reclaiming a resurfaced floor is a festival and a labour both; the salt must be scraped, the rot burned out, the barnacles knocked from a grandmother's doorstep.

## Who Reads and Rules the Water

Above it all rises the [[beacon-spire]], the one tower the flood never claims, from whose top the tide is read and signalled to every tier below. There the [[tidewright-guild|Tidewright Guild]] keeps its watch, predicting the water's coming and rebuilding to meet it. Down at the low ground stands [[the-saltworks]], where the salt is boiled and the tide-reader's clouded glass is made. And out on the shifting mid-tide floats the [[reef-market]], lashed together from hulls and rafts, where goods change hands between tiers and the flood is simply weather to be traded through. Cael Morren is not a place that fights the sea. It is a place that has made a life of yielding to it — patient, salt-worn, and quietly unbroken.
