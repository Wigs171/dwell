# The Drowned Oracle

## The woman who breathes the water

At the base of [[the-undercity|the Undercity]], where the old bell-tower stands forever half-drowned, lives a woman who does not need to surface for air. Whether she was born to it or made by it, no one agrees; the Ferrymen who bring her salvage and questions say only that she opens her eyes underwater and looks back at you calmly, as though the flooded dark were her element and the dry world the strange one. She has no name anyone uses. She is the Drowned Oracle, and the Undercity's drowned lanes lead, eventually, to her door.

## Prophecy in the bell's voice

She keeps the tower of the [[drowning-bells|drowning bells]], and her speech seems to come from them as much as from her — a low, resonant certainty about the coming water, its hours and its heights, delivered in fragments that reward patience and punish the literal-minded. Tidewrights read the tide with instruments; the Oracle claims simply to overhear it. Whether her prophecy is true foresight or the drowned bells' physics translated into words, those who have wagered their lives on her turnings have more often lived than not.

## What she hears in the deep

The bell-tower stands nearest of all the Undercity to [[the-deepworks|the Deepworks]], the ancient engine grinding beneath, and the Oracle speaks of it the way a sleeper speaks of a heartbeat felt through a wall — always present, rarely heeded. She understands [[the-long-tide|the Long Tide]] not as a cycle to be counted but as a great sunk voice, and she has begun, in recent turnings, to say that the voice is changing. Something below is no longer keeping its old measure.

## Sought and feared

The Anchorites would give much to know what she hears; the Tidewrights half-believe and half-dread her; the Ferrymen treat her with the wary reverence owed to weather. She takes no side in the city's arguments and gives no clean answers, only the tide's own — which are true, and terrible, and never quite in time to be safe. To descend to the Drowned Oracle is to ask the sea directly, and to accept that the sea does not comfort.

Those who have knelt at the bell-tower longest say her tide-speech circles one thing she will not finish: [[the-ninth-prophecy]], spoken once at the last low ebb and never written down.
