# Sabel Course

## Keeper of the record

Sabel Course holds the highest office of the [[tidewright-guild]]: Master, and warden of the [[the-tide-ledger|Tide-Ledger]], the great salt-stained book in which every reading of the water has been set down since the founding. He is a man made of margins and cross-references, who trusts the accumulated centuries over any single clever eye. To Sabel the Ledger is not a record but a covenant — the promise that the tide, whatever it does, can be known if only one is humble and patient enough to keep counting.

## The teacher's dilemma

He took [[maren-vote|Maren Vote]] as his apprentice knowing her gift outran his method, and he has never quite decided whether that gift is a blessing to the Guild or a threat to it. He teaches her the discipline of the instruments precisely because he distrusts the feeling in her teeth; a reading that cannot be written down, he says, cannot be defended. When Maren first pointed to the crooked columns and named the crisis, Sabel's instinct was to check the arithmetic a hundred times before he would say aloud what she already believed.

## Faith in the long count

Sabel understands the tide as [[the-long-tide|the Long Tide]] has always been understood — a vast, slow, faithful thing, forty years in its breathing, older than grief and more reliable. He carries in memory the shape of the [[the-first-sinking|First Sinking]], the catastrophe that drowned the old town and taught the first Tidewrights to read rather than resist. That lesson is his whole creed: you do not stop the water, you learn it, and you build to survive it.

## When the ledger lies

The tragedy closing on Sabel Course is that his covenant may be breaking. If the readings truly no longer hold, then a lifetime of faithful counting has been counting the wrong thing, and the Ledger — his covenant, his defence — has begun to lie. He is too honest to ignore this and too cautious to act on it quickly, and in the gap between those two virtues the tide keeps rising early. He would give much to be wrong, and fears, in the small hours, that his apprentice is right.
