# The First Sinking

## The Night the Streets Went Under

Generations ago the old town stood dry and whole upon the flats, ignorant of the sea's true nature. Then, in a span the songs remember as a single terrible season, the water rose and did not stop. Cellars filled, then hearths, then the sills of upper windows; the harbour bells rang and rang until the ringers themselves were driven up into the towers. When at last the water settled, the town it had swallowed was gone from the air entirely. This is the [[the-first-sinking|First Sinking]] as the world names it — the moment the ground learned it could not be trusted.

## The Engine Wakes

The Sinking was no storm and no flood of ordinary rain. Beneath the drowned streets the [[the-deepworks|Deepworks]] had stirred — the great half-understood machine that had lain quiet beneath the flats since before the town was raised. Whether some careless hand woke it or whether it simply reached the appointed hour of its long design, none can say. What is certain is that its turning drew the sea in and would not release it, and that its slow labour has never ceased since. The catastrophe was not an ending but a beginning: the first stroke of an engine that has beaten ever after.

## Birth of the Undercity

What the water took, it kept, and so was made the [[the-undercity|Undercity]] — the old town preserved whole beneath the flood, its lanes and lintels intact under the weight of the sea. Barnacle and silt have claimed it, and drowned bells hang silent in its towers until the turns of the tide, but it is not rubble. It waits. At the deep ebb it surfaces again, and the living walk the streets their ancestors fled, reading the marks the drowning left on the stone.

## The Rhythm It Set

From that one sinking came the pattern the whole world now lives by. The Sinking did not simply drown a town; it set the first beat of the [[the-long-tide|Long Tide]], the forty-year breath of rise and fall that orders every life in [[cael-morren|Cael Morren]]. The city that rose from the ruin was built to drown and resurface without dying, tier upon tier, having learned in one dreadful season the lesson it has never since forgotten: the water always comes back.
