# Saltglass

## The Glass That Feels the Water

Saltglass is a pale, faintly greenish glass that answers to the sea before the sea arrives. Left in a still room, a plate of it will cloud from its edges inward as the water gathers unseen leagues away, then clear again as the flood recedes — a mist upon its face that thickens and thins with the breath of the [[the-long-tide|Long Tide]]. No lens, no mechanism, only the glass itself, sweating the tide's coming days or seasons before any wave shows on the flats. It is the tide-reader's chief instrument, and a good plate is worth a house.

## Poured at the Saltworks

The making is close and jealously kept. Saltglass is cast only at the [[the-saltworks|Saltworks]], where brine is boiled and the salt of the deep water is worked into the melt while it is still fire. The proportion, the cooling, the long slow annealing — these are craft secrets, and a badly poured plate lies, clouding at the wrong hour and drowning those who trusted it. The best sheets ring like a bell when struck and hold their reading true across an entire turn of the tide.

## Reading the Cloud

A plate of glass is useless without the eye to read it, and so saltglass is the working heart of [[reading-the-tide|reading the tide]] — the patient craft of watching the mist creep and knowing what it foretells. A reader learns the plate's every flaw, the exact grey that means a season and the grey that means a year. What the glass shows, the reader speaks; what the reader speaks is written down and kept, plate against plate across the generations, in the great master record of every reading — the [[the-tide-ledger|Tide-Ledger]].

## From Hand to Hand

Because a true plate is scarce and dear, saltglass passes constantly through the [[reef-market|Reef Market]], the floating market of the mid-tide, where readers, smugglers, and desperate households bid for a clear pane against the coming flood. Cracked plates are sold cheap to the credulous; forged ones, cast with common salt, are the market's oldest swindle. In the Quickening now breaking over the city, even honest glass has begun to cloud strangely, clearing when it should darken — and every reader who has seen it knows the fault is not in the glass.
