# The Drowning Bells

## Bells Beneath the Water

In the towers of the [[the-undercity|Undercity]] hang the old town's bells, drowned where they were rung. They did not fall silent when the water took them. At the turns of the tide — when the sea leans over from rising to falling, or from falling to rising — the bells sound, deep and slow beneath the flood, a tolling that carries not through the air but through the water and up through the wet stone into the living tiers above. Folk who have felt it in their teeth and their bones do not forget it. The city calls them the Drowning Bells.

## What They Have Rung Since the Sinking

The bells have tolled at every turn since the [[the-first-sinking|First Sinking]], when the water climbed the towers and the ringers were driven up before it. Some say the last ringers never left, and that it is their hands still working the ropes in the dark; others say the tide itself is the ringer now, that the moving sea sets the great bronze swinging. Either way, the Drowning Bells keep the beat of the [[the-long-tide|Long Tide]] more faithfully than any glass or ledger. When they sound, the tide has surely turned, and the whole city knows it.

## The Oracle at the Bell-Tower

At the drowned bell-tower dwells the [[the-drowned-oracle|Drowned Oracle]], the woman who breathes the water and does not rise. She lives among the bells, in the flooded belfry, and it is said she reads their tolling as a tide-reader reads a glass — that the bronze speaks to her, and she speaks its meaning in prophecy. Those brave or desperate enough to be ferried down to her come back with words they only half understand, spoken to the rhythm of a bell they could not hear.

## The Bells Ring Wrong

Of late the Drowning Bells have begun to toll out of season. They sound when no turn is due, once, twice, at hours no reading foretold, their bronze voices ringing up through the tiers to wake the sleeping city with a dread it cannot name. Every reader who has heard them knows what it means: the tide's beat is broken, and the bells, honest as they have always been, are only telling the truth of it. What the glass whispers and the ledger doubts, the bells now ring aloud.
