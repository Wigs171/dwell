# Reading the Tide

## The Central Craft

To read the tide is to answer the only question Cael Morren has ever truly asked: when will the water come, and how high? It is the discipline on which the whole tiered city depends, the skill that decides when a storey is surrendered and when it is scoured clean and reclaimed. Mastered by the [[tidewright-guild|Tidewright Guild]], reading is neither prophecy nor arithmetic alone but something between — a trained attention to a hundred small signs that, taken together, foretell the flood.

## The Signs and the Instruments

A reader watches the wet line on old stone, the temper of the wells, the way barnacles rise or die back on a mooring-post. The reader's surest instrument is [[saltglass]], the pane that clouds as the water gathers and clears as it recedes, so that a wright carries the tide's mood at their hip. But no single glass is trusted alone. Every reading is weighed against [[the-tide-ledger|the Tide-Ledger]], the master record in which centuries of prior readings agree or dissent, and confirmed against the signals thrown from the [[beacon-spire|Beacon Spire]], whose lantern turns a lone wright's judgment into a warning the whole city can obey.

## The Long Rhythm and the Break

True reading is not the work of a day. Because the water moves on [[the-long-tide|the Long Tide]] — a rise and fall spanning some forty years — a reader must think across a human lifetime, learning to feel a slow swell that no single season reveals. The best readers seem almost to breathe with it. Yet the craft rests on one silent faith: that the tide keeps its old patterns, that what came before will come again. That faith is now failing. Readings that held for centuries are breaking against the water's new impatience, and the wrights who trusted the signs find, for the first time, that the tide has begun to lie.
