# Who Keeps the Tide

Every city has a way of surviving. Cael Morren has three, and they do not agree, and the disagreement has never mattered more than now — because the [[the-quickening|Quickening]] has made the old truce between them suddenly expensive.

## Read It

The [[tidewright-guild|Tidewright Guild]] holds that the drowning cannot be beaten, only *known*. Their whole craft — [[reading-the-tide|reading the tide]], the clouding of [[saltglass]], the centuries of numbers kept in the [[the-tide-ledger|Tide-Ledger]] — exists to answer a single question early enough to act on it: when will the water come, and how far? To a Tidewright, a house is not a permanent thing but a well-timed one. Rebuild low when the ebb is long; abandon low before the flood; keep the [[beacon-spire|Beacon Spire]] lit and the ledger honest. Their virtue is humility before the water. Their weakness is that humility offers no comfort when the numbers themselves begin to lie.

## Stop It

The [[the-anchorites|Anchorites]] find that humility unbearable. Why should a whole people spend their lives moving their beds upstairs and down, they ask, when the thing that drowns them is a *machine* — and machines can be stilled? Under [[castellan-brine|Castellan Brine]] they have made a quiet study of the [[the-deepworks|Deepworks]], convinced that the tide is not fate but mechanism, and that mechanism can be stopped. They would make Cael Morren permanent: dry streets, deep foundations, an end to the drowning forever. Their virtue is hope. Their danger is that no one truly knows what the Deepworks does, or what stopping it would cost — and the Quickening may be the first sign of what happens when it is disturbed.

## Ride It

The [[the-ferrymen|Ferrymen]] want the tide neither read into submission nor stopped. The flux is their whole living: they move goods and people through the changing water, between the tiers, down into the flooded lanes of the [[the-undercity|Undercity]] that only they can navigate. A stilled tide would beach them; a perfectly predicted one would put them out of a trade. So they thrive on exactly the uncertainty the others fear. Their virtue is freedom and nerve. Their weakness is that they profit most from a world in danger — and the Quickening, which terrifies everyone else, has been very good for business.

## The Stakes Now

While the tide behaved, the three could coexist: one read it, one studied it, one rode it. The Quickening ends that. If the tide is truly breaking, then reading it may no longer be enough, stopping it may be the only rescue *or* the final catastrophe, and riding it may be the last freedom before the water stops keeping any promise at all.

All three factions, when pressed, trace their charters back to [[the-first-tidewright]] — a figure each of them describes differently, and none can prove existed.
