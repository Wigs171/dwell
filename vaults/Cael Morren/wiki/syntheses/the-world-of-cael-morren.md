# The World of Cael Morren

There is a city that has made its peace with drowning. [[cael-morren|Cael Morren]] does not stand against the sea the way other cities do, with walls and pumps and stubbornness. It stands *with* it — built in tiers, its lower storeys plastered and shuttered and left to be taken, its upper storeys waiting empty for the years when the water will drive everyone upward. The people do not ask whether the flood will come. They ask only *when*, and by how much, and they have built an entire civilisation around the answer.

## The Rhythm

The answer is the [[the-long-tide|Long Tide]] — not the daily lapping of an ordinary sea but a vast slow breath, rising and falling across roughly forty years. At its lowest ebb the water withdraws far enough that the [[the-undercity|Undercity]], the drowned town of an earlier age, lifts back into the light, barnacled and streaming, walkable for a single strange season before the sea reclaims it. To live here is to know that everything you build will one day be underwater, and that everything underwater was once somebody's home.

The tide is not the sea's own idea. Beneath the Undercity turns the [[the-deepworks|Deepworks]], a machine older than memory, and it is the Deepworks — not the moon, not the wind — that pulls the water up and lets it down. The [[the-first-sinking|First Sinking]] was the day it first pulled the old town under. No one built it. No one can stop it. Until now, no one has badly needed to.

## Three Answers

Faced with a world that drowns, the people of Cael Morren have split into three answers, and their quarrel is the quarrel of the whole world. The [[tidewright-guild|Tidewrights]] read the water and rebuild to it, trusting prediction over permanence. The [[the-anchorites|Anchorites]] refuse the drowning altogether and study the Deepworks in hope of stilling it. The [[the-ferrymen|Ferrymen]] want nothing stilled at all, for the flux is their trade and their freedom. To ask *who keeps the tide* is to ask who should own the future — a question laid out in full in [[who-keeps-the-tide]].

## The Break

All of it rested on one assumption: that the tide could be trusted to keep its schedule. That assumption is now breaking. The [[the-quickening|Quickening]] has begun — the water arriving early, arriving unevenly, breaking readings that held for three hundred years. Somewhere in the ledgers a young apprentice was the first to see the numbers slip. Whatever the Deepworks is doing, it is no longer doing it on time, and a city that survives by knowing *when* has suddenly stopped knowing.
